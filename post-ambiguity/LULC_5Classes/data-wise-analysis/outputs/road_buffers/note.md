In professional GIS analysis, it is extremely common to use a single "Baseline Infrastructure" dataset (like GRIP4). Major state and national highways—which are the primary drivers of the "ribbon development" you are analyzing—have existed for decades.

While you might miss a few brand-new local roads built between 2020 and 2025, using the GRIP4 snapshot as a static baseline to measure how urban pixels "fill in" the 1km, 3km, and 5km buffers over a decade is a completely valid and mathematically sound methodology for your assignment.

In urban planning and GIS studies, infrastructure does not cause instantaneous urbanization; there is a temporal lag.

    A highway built or expanded in 2016 will not instantly spawn a dense urban corridor by 2017. The commercial and residential "fill-in" happens over the next 5 to 10 years.

Urban expansion at a state-wide level (Bihar is roughly 94,000 km²) is dictated by macro-infrastructure (National Highways, State Highways, major bridges).

    These massive transit corridors take decades to plan and build, and they almost never move once established.

State-level 'ribbon development' is anchored by major arterial highways, not local neighborhood streets. The macro-infrastructure of Bihar has remained geographically static over this decade
